# Axiom 4: Only the Shadow Exists

&gt; **We never interact with the object itself. We interact only with its projections.**  
&gt; Like a two-dimensional world cannot see a sphere — it sees only circles of different diameters. Physical laws may be cross-sections of the object's structure.

---

## Author's Commentary (lordekz)

This is the axiom of **epistemic humility** — but taken to its extreme.

We are used to thinking that if we study something deeply enough, we eventually grasp its true nature. Physics has given us extraordinary predictive power. We can land a rover on Mars. We can predict the behavior of particles to twelve decimal places. Surely we are touching reality?

This axiom says: no. What we call "physical laws" are not the object. They are **cross-sections** — projections, shadows, slices. They are real, they are consistent, they are useful. But they are not the object.

### The sphere and the flatland

Edwin Abbott's *Flatland* is the classic analogy. A two-dimensional being sees a sphere passing through its plane as a point that grows into a circle, then shrinks back to a point. The flatlander never sees the sphere. It sees a **sequence of circles** and may infer: "There is something three-dimensional." But it cannot verify this directly. Its entire physics — the rules governing those circles — is a projection of something it cannot access.

We may be flatlanders. Our four-dimensional spacetime may be a "plane" through something else. Our physical laws — conservation of energy, entropy increase, quantum mechanics — may be the "circles" we see.

### Why this is not instrumentalism

Instrumentalism says: "Scientific theories are just tools for prediction. We should not ask if they are 'true.'" This is a philosophical position about the status of theories.

This axiom says something different. It says: "There exists something real. Our theories capture real patterns — **projections** of that something. But the something itself is not among the projections." This is a **realist** claim, not an anti-realist one. It affirms the reality of the object while denying our access to it.

### Physical laws as cross-sections

Consider:
- Conservation of energy: a symmetry of our projection
- Entropy increase: a directional feature of our projection
- Quantum uncertainty: a limit on the resolution of our projection
- Speed of light $c$: the maximum rate at which information can propagate within our projection

None of these need be properties of the object. They may be **artifacts of the projection mechanism** — like the way a photograph flattens depth, or the way a shadow loses color.

### The danger of this axiom

This is where the theory risks becoming unfalsifiable. If everything is a "projection," then any evidence can be dismissed as "just a shadow." To avoid this, the theory must make **specific claims** about what projections should look like — and what they should not.

This is addressed in Axiom 6: the claim that all fundamental limits are manifestations of one horizon. This is a unifying claim that can be tested: do these limits share structural features? Are they derivable from a common principle?

## Formal Sketch

Let $O$ be the object in its "full" (unprojected) state. Let $P_\theta(O)$ be a projection of $O$ onto a subspace parameterized by $\theta$.

**Axiom 4 states:**

$$\forall \text{ observers } S: \quad S \text{ interacts only with } P_\theta(O) \text{ for some } \theta$$

And:

$$\nexists S: \quad S \text{ interacts with } O \text{ directly}$$

Physical laws $L$ are conjectured to satisfy:

$$L \approx \frac{\partial P_\theta(O)}{\partial \theta}$$

That is, physical laws are approximations of how the projection changes with the projection parameter. This is speculative but provides a direction for formalization.
