# Connection: Quantum Uncertainty

> Heisenberg's uncertainty principle as a possible manifestation of the Information Horizon.

---

## What Quantum Mechanics Says

The Heisenberg uncertainty principle:

$$\Delta x \cdot \Delta p \geq \frac{\hbar}{2}$$

Conjugate variables (position/momentum, time/energy) cannot be simultaneously measured with arbitrary precision. This is not a measurement limitation in the classical sense — it is a **fundamental property of quantum systems**.

## The Parallel with Axiom 2

Axiom 2: Information has a local limit. The object exceeds it.  
Quantum mechanics: Information about conjugate variables has a joint limit.

In both cases, **complete information is impossible** for a local observer. The difference is scale:

- Quantum uncertainty: applies to all quantum systems
- Information Horizon: applies specifically to the object

## The Measurement Problem

When you measure a quantum system, you become entangled with it. The observer and observed form a joint system. The "collapse" (in Copenhagen interpretation) or branching (in many-worlds) means the observer's state is restructured by the information gained.

This resembles Axiom 1: the system (observer) is changed by obtaining information. But in quantum mechanics, the change is reversible in principle (decoherence can be reversed). The Information Horizon proposes an **irreversible** restructuring.

## Speculative Extension

What if quantum uncertainty is not a property of "quantum systems" but a property of **any system interacting with projections of the object**?

In this view:
- Quantum mechanics describes the "fine structure" of projections
- Classical mechanics emerges at larger scales where projection artifacts average out
- The uncertainty principle is the "pixelation" of reality at the projection boundary
